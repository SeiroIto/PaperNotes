Summary

  • Previous studies have used English and math exam scores directly. Measurement errors and multicollinearity undermined the research.
  • A factor model (Cunha, Heckman, and Schennach 2010Cunha, Flavio, James J. Heckman, and Susanne M. Schennach. 2010. Estimating the Technology of Cognitive and Noncognitive Skill Formation. Econometrica 78 (3): 883931. https://doi.org/10.3982/ECTA6551.) is used to extract math and verbal skills and to reduce measurement errors and multicollinearity.
  • Massive data of .5 million students with 30 subject exams from UK are used.
    Math on lagged math (elasticities .64-1.28), lagged verbal (0-.11). Verbal on lagged verbal (.80-1.06).
  • Math skills only contribute to future math skills. Verbal skills contrubute to both future verbal and math skills.
    College enrollment returns by verbal vs.math skills: .150 vs..057 for females, .127 vs..061 for males.
  • Marginal returns to college enrollment on verbal skills are larger than math skills (2-3 times).
  • This happens all the students except for top 20% of males who show similar marginal returns on both skills.
  • Improving school quality to outstanding grade can increase college enrollment rate by 4.5% points.
  • Policy should aim: Verbal skills and school quality improvement.
  • Females have higher verbal skills than males. This can explain the college enrollment gender gaps in many countries.
  • Previous studies used English exam scores with school achievement. Major part of verbal skills was subsumed in school achiervement and resulted in near zero estimates on verbal skills.
  • Two-stage estimation procedure is developed to maximise a complex nonlinear likelihood.

Biases in OLS estimators using error-ridden correlated variables

Errors in variables are \[ X_{1}=x_{1}+u_{1}, \quad X_{2}=x_{2}+u_{2},\quad \mbox{cov}[x_{1}, x_{2}]\neq 0, \quad u_{1}\perp u_{2}, \] where we only observe \(X\) but not true \(x\) in \[ Y_{i}=\beta_{0}+\beta_{1}X_{1i}+\beta_{2}X_{2i}+u_{i}. \]

  • One can show OLS estimates give \(\hat{\beta}_{1}\simeq \hat{\beta}_{2}\) no matter true values of \(\beta_{1}, \beta_{2}\), when correlation of two covariates becomes 1 and signal-to-noise ratios are similar.
  • Authors write that factor models can clean up measurement errors and correlations. By design, factors are modeled as orthogonal given unobserved individual random effects, and factors are estimated, so when they are estimated imprecisely, it manifests the problem with large standard errors, which is not the case in EIV-OLS.

Denote \(\lambda_{j}=\frac{\mbox{$\mathcal V$}[u_{j}]}{\mbox{$\mathcal V$}[X_{j}]}=\frac{\mbox{$\mathcal V$}[u_{j}]}{\mbox{$\mathcal V$}[x_{j}]+\mbox{$\mathcal V$}[u_{j}]}\) for \(j=1,2\). Madala (1992Madala, G. S. 1992. Introduction to Econometrics. 2nd ed. McMillan.), 11.9 gives formulae for EIV-OLS estimators in the two variable case: \[\begin{equation} \begin{aligned} \text{plim}\ \hat{\beta}_{1} &= \beta_{1}-\frac{\lambda_{1}\beta_{1}-\rho\lambda_{2}\beta_{2}}{1-\rho^{2}},\\ \text{plim}\ \hat{\beta}_{2} &= \beta_{2}-\frac{\lambda_{2}\beta_{2}-\rho\lambda_{1}\beta_{1}}{1-\rho^{2}}. \end{aligned} \tag{11.9} \end{equation}\] We see that \[ \lambda_{j}\left(\mbox{$\mathcal V$}[x_{j}]+\mbox{$\mathcal V$}[u_{j}]\right)=\mbox{$\mathcal V$}[u_{j}] \quad \Leftrightarrow\quad\frac{\mbox{$\mathcal V$}[u_{j}]}{\mbox{$\mathcal V$}[x_{j}]}=\frac{\lambda_{j}}{1-\lambda_{j}}, \] so \[ \mbox{$\mathcal V$}[x_{j}]+\mbox{$\mathcal V$}[u_{j}]=\mbox{$\mathcal V$}[x_{j}]\left(1+\frac{\lambda_{j}}{1-\lambda_{j}}\right)=\mbox{$\mathcal V$}[x_{j}]\frac{1}{1-\lambda_{j}}. \] By assumption \(\mbox{cov}[X_{1}, X_{2}]=\mbox{cov}[x_{1}+x_{2}]\), the correlation is \[ \begin{aligned} \frac{\mbox{cov}[X_{1}, X_{2}]}{\left\{\mbox{$\mathcal V$}_{X_{1}}\mbox{$\mathcal V$}_{X_{2}}\right\}^{\frac{1}{2}}} &= \frac{\mbox{cov}[x_{1}+x_{2}]}{\left\{\left(\mbox{$\mathcal V$}_{x_{1}}+\mbox{$\mathcal V$}_{u_{1}}\right)\left(\mbox{$\mathcal V$}_{x_{2}}+\mbox{$\mathcal V$}_{u_{2}}\right)\right\}^{\frac{1}{2}}} = \frac{\mbox{cov}[x_{1}+x_{2}]}{\left\{\mbox{$\mathcal V$}_{x_{1}}\left(1-\lambda_{1}\right)^{-1}\mbox{$\mathcal V$}_{x_{2}}\left(1-\lambda_{2}\right)^{-1}\right\}^{\frac{1}{2}}},\\ \rho &= \rho^{*}\sqrt{\left(1-\lambda_{1}\right)\left(1-\lambda_{2}\right)}. \end{aligned} \] where \(\rho^{*}=\mbox{corr}[x_{1}, x_{2}]\).

Setting \(\lambda_{1}=\lambda_{2}=\lambda\), plug these and take limits \(\rho^{*}\rightarrow 1\): Authors show it goes to 1, not -1. Not sure where I got it wrong. \[ \begin{aligned} \frac{\text{plim}\ \hat{\beta}_{1}}{\text{plim}\ \hat{\beta}_{2}} &= \frac{\{1-\rho^{*2}(1-\lambda)^{2}\}\beta_{1}-\lambda\{\beta_{1}-\rho^{*}(1-\lambda)\beta_{2}\}}{\{1-\rho^{*2}(1-\lambda)^{2}\}\beta_{2}-\lambda\{\beta_{2}-\rho^{*}(1-\lambda)\beta_{1}\}},\\ \lim_{\rho^{*}\rightarrow 1} \frac{\text{plim}\ \hat{\beta}_{1}}{\text{plim}\ \hat{\beta}_{2}} &= \frac{\lambda^{2}\beta_{1}-\lambda\{\beta_{1}-(1-\lambda)\beta_{2}\}}{\lambda^{2}\beta_{2}-\lambda\{\beta_{2}-(1-\lambda)\beta_{1}\}},\\ &= \frac{\lambda(1-\lambda)\left(\beta_{2}-\beta_{1}\right)}{\lambda(1-\lambda)\left(\beta_{1}-\beta_{2}\right)},\\ &=-1. \end{aligned} \]

Model

Production function

Skill \(\Theta^{k}_{it}\) production function of subject \(k\in\{m, v\}\) at \(t=\{2, 3, 4\}\) \[ \begin{aligned} \Theta^{k}_{it} &= A^{k}_{it}\left[\delta^{k}_{t}\left(\Omega^{k}_{it}\right)^{\rho^{k}_{t}}+(1-\delta^{k}_{t})\left(Q_{it}\right)^{\rho^{k}_{t}}\right]^{\frac{r^{k}_{t}}{\rho^{k}_{t}}},\\ &= A^{k}_{it}\bigg[\delta^{k}_{t}\left\{\alpha^{k}_{t}\left(\Theta^{m}_{it-1}\right)^{\gamma^{k}_{t}}+(1-\alpha^{k}_{t})\left(\Theta^{v}_{it-1}\right)^{\gamma^{k}_{t}}\right\}^{\frac{\rho^{k}_{t}}{\gamma^{k}_{t}}}+(1-\delta^{k}_{t})\left(Q_{it}\right)^{\rho^{k}_{t}}\bigg]^{\frac{r^{k}_{t}}{\rho^{k}_{t}}}. \end{aligned} \] Denote \(\ln\Theta^{k}_{it}=\theta^{k}_{it}\).

  • Skill aggregator \(\Omega^{k}_{it}\) (can be thought as non-TFP general skill level) nests another level of CES aggregation. By doing so, elasticities of substitution can vary across inputs by periods.

TFP \[ A^{k}_{it}=e^{\psi^{k}_{t}x_{it}+\pi^{k}_{i}+\nu^{k}_{it}}, \quad \nu^{k}_{it}\sim N(0, \zeta^{2}_{tk}). \] \(x_{it}\) is a vector of individual observables and neighbourhood FEs, \(\pi^{k}_{i}\) is individual skill specific REs, and \(\nu^{k}_{it}\) is an idiosyncratic shock to skill production.

Measurement system

Scores/measures and factors \(\theta'_{i} = \left( \begin{array}{ccccc} \theta^{m}_{i1} & \theta^{v}_{i1}& \dots &\theta^{m}_{i4}& \theta^{v}_{i4} \end{array} \right)\) with factor loading \(\lambda'_{j}= \left( \begin{array}{ccccc} \lambda^{m}_{1}& \lambda^{v}_{1}& \dots &\lambda^{m}_{4}& \lambda^{v}_{4} \end{array} \right)\): \[ w^{*}_{ij}=\mu_{j}+\lambda'_{j}\theta_{i}+\eta_{ij}, \quad \eta_{ij}\sim \mathcal N(0, \sigma^{2}_{j}), \ \theta_{i}\sim MVN(\xi, \Delta). \] Factors \(\theta\) are assumed to be distributed as multivariate normal.

  • Factor loadings of each outcomes are estimated on intercept, concurrent math and/or verbal while other period math/verbal are set to 0. For math/verbal tests, loadings on concurrent math/verbal are set to 1, others to 0 (see Table O4).

Exam self-selection for subject \(c\) at key stage 4: Take an exam if \(a^{*}_{ic}>0\). \[ a^{*}_{ic}=\tau_{c0}+\tau_{c1}\theta^{m}_{3}+\tau_{c2}\theta^{v}_{3}+\tau_{c4}\theta^{sel}_{i,4}+v_{ic}, \quad v_{ic}\sim\Lambda(0, 1). \] \(\theta^{sel}_{i,4}\) is a factor that affirms intellectual challenges that are common for all subjects. \(v_{ic}\) is distributed as logistic with mean zero, scale 1.

Estimation

Likelihood and two-step estimation

First stage: Estimate measurement system parameters.
Second stage: Estimate CES (production function) parameters.

Log likelihood of \(n\) students \[\begin{equation} \begin{aligned} LL(\Psi) &=\sum_{i=1}^{n}\ln L\left(w_{i}, a_{i}, x_{i}, Q_{i}\right),\\ &=\sum_{i=1}^{n}\ln \int_{\theta}L\left(w_{i}, a_{i}, x_{i}, Q_{i}\left|\theta\right.\right)d\theta,\\ &\propto\sum_{i=1}^{n}\ln \left[\int_{\theta} p\left(w_{i}, a_{i}\left|\theta\right.\right)F\left(\theta\left| x_{i}, Q_{i}\right.\right)d\theta\right], \quad [ F\left(\theta, x_{i}, Q_{i}\right)=F\left(\theta\left| x_{i}, Q_{i}\right.\right) F\left(x_{i}, Q_{i}\right), \ \text{drop }F\left(x_{i}, Q_{i}\right)]\\ &=\sum_{i=1}^{n}\ln \left[\int_{\theta} p\left(w_{i}, a_{i}\left|\theta\right.\right)\frac{p\left(x_{i}, Q_{i}\left|\theta \right.\right)p(\theta)}{p\left(x_{i}, Q_{i}\right)}d\theta\right], \quad \left[F\left(\theta\left| x_{i}, Q_{i}\right.\right)=\frac{F\left( x_{i}, Q_{i}\left|\theta\right.\right)F(\theta)}{F\left(x_{i}, Q_{i}\right)}\right] \\ &=\sum_{i=1}^{n}\ln \left[\int_{\theta} \frac{p\left(w_{i}, a_{i}\right)}{p\left(w_{i}, a_{i}\right)}p\left(w_{i}, a_{i}\left|\theta\right.\right)p(\theta)\frac{p\left(x_{i}, Q_{i}\left|\theta \right.\right)}{p\left(x_{i}, Q_{i}\right)}d\theta\right],\\ &=\sum_{i=1}^{n}\ln \left[\int_{\theta} h\left(\theta\left|\right.w_{i}, a_{i}\right)p\left(w_{i}, a_{i}\right)\frac{p\left(x_{i}, Q_{i}\left|\theta \right.\right)}{p\left(x_{i}, Q_{i}\right)}d\theta\right],\\ &= \underbrace{\sum_{i=1}^{n}\ln \left[\int_{\theta} p\left(w_{i}, a_{i}\left|\theta\right.\right)p(\theta)d\theta\right]}_{\text{first stage for }\theta, \lambda, \tau, \mu, \sigma^{2}} +\sum_{i=1}^{n}\ln \left[\int_{\theta} p\left(x_{i}, Q_{i}\left|\theta\right.\right)h\left(\theta\left|w_{i}, a_{i}\right.\right)d\theta\right] - \sum_{i=1}^{n}\ln \left[p\left(x_{i}, Q_{i}\right)\right],\\ \end{aligned} \tag{4} \end{equation}\] Authors say maximising over the first term gives unbiased and consistent estimates. Would not the second term be necessay? Authors maximize the first stage part over \(\lambda, \tau, \mu, \sigma^{2}\) given simulated \(\theta\) while ignoring the rest. They write that ignoring the second term may give rise to inefficiency, but simplifies estimation. \[ \mbox{LL}_{\text{first}\\ \text{stage}}= \sum_{i=1}^{n}\ln \left[\int_{\theta} p\left(w_{i}, a_{i}\left|\theta\right.\right)p(\theta)d\theta\right] \] To estimate CES parameters, it is useful to consider the score (gradient of likelihood). MLE is a root of the score. Score is given by: \[\begin{equation} \begin{aligned} \frac{\partial LL(\Psi)}{\partial \Psi} &= \sum_{i=1}^{n}\frac{1}{L\left(w_{i}, a_{i}, x_{i}, Q_{i}\right)}\int_{\theta}\frac{\partial p\left(w_{i}, a_{i}\left|\theta\right.\right)F\left(\theta\left| x_{i}, Q_{i}\right.\right)}{\partial \Psi}d\theta,\\ &= \sum_{i=1}^{n}\int_{\theta}\frac{p\left(w_{i}, a_{i}\left|\theta\right.\right)F\left(\theta\left| x_{i}, Q_{i}\right.\right)}{L\left(w_{i}, a_{i}, x_{i}, Q_{i}\right)}\frac{\partial \ln \left[p\left(w_{i}, a_{i}\left|\theta\right.\right)F\left(\theta\left| x_{i}, Q_{i}\right.\right)\right]}{\partial \Psi}d\theta,\\ &= \sum_{i=1}^{n}\int_{\theta}h\left(\theta\left|w_{i}, a_{i}, x_{i}, Q_{i}\right.\right) \frac{\partial \ln \left[p\left(w_{i}, a_{i}\left|\theta\right.\right)F\left(\theta\left| x_{i}, Q_{i}\right.\right)\right]}{\partial \Psi}d\theta,\\ &= \sum_{i=1}^{n}\int_{\theta} \frac{\partial \ln \left[p\left(w_{i}, a_{i}\left|\theta\right.\right)\right]}{\partial \Psi}h\left(\theta\left|w_{i}, a_{i}, x_{i}, Q_{i}\right.\right)d\theta +\underbrace{\sum_{i=1}^{n}\int_{\theta} \frac{\partial \ln \left[F\left(\theta\left| x_{i}, Q_{i}\right.\right)\right]}{\partial \Psi}h\left(\theta\left|w_{i}, a_{i}, x_{i}, Q_{i}\right.\right)d\theta}_{\text{second stage for }\alpha, \delta, \gamma, \rho, \psi, \pi},\\ \end{aligned} \end{equation}\] If one finds the CES roots \(\alpha, \delta, \gamma, \rho, \psi, \pi\) of the 2nd term, one can maximise the conditional likelihood (conditional on \(\theta\)). Results

Factors

Fig.1: KS4 factor distributions

Summary statistics (Table 2)

  • Factors increase with grade, which is expected by design of National Curriculum scores.
  • Female-Male differences are small in maths, but are large in verbal and widen with the grade.

Dynamics (Table 3)

  • Self-productivity \(\frac{\partial \theta^{k}_{it}}{\partial \theta^{k}_{it-1}}\) is large.
  • Cross productivity \(\frac{\partial \theta^{m}_{it}}{\partial \theta^{v}_{it-1}}\) is nonzero only for verbal but small (8.4% of self-productivity for KS4 females, 2.5% for KS4 males).
  • Controlling for time-invariant individual random effects in TFP reduces estimates.
  • Improvements in school quality (satisfactory \(\rightarrow\) good) increases estimated factors, e.g., increases estimates by 1.082 points of math factor at KS2.

Other outcomes, gender gaps

College enrollment (Table 4, 6, Figure 2)

Skill investments are endogenous. Earlier skills (KS2, age 7), less likely to be endogenous to college enrollment, was used and show similar results (Table OA17).

(G)ender differences in verbal skills play an important role in the gender gaps in college enrollment. STEM fields: Gender gaps in skills explained (only) 35% of observed gaps.

  • Verbal skills play a larger role than math skills.
  • Increasing verbal skills increases college enrollment more than math skills.
  • Increasing math skills at low skill levels does not increase college enrollment for males.
  • Increasing mean male verbal skills to female level wipes out gender gap in college enrollment (Table 6).
  • Increasing school quality (to outstanding) increases college enrollment 4.5% points (10.9%) for females and 4.5% points (13.8%) for males (Table 7).

University selection (Table 5)

Conditional on STEM enrollment, there are smaller variations in math skills which tend to increase estimated response.

  • Verbal skills are more related than math skills in: Nonselective college enrollment, graduation.
  • Males math and verbal skill responses are similar for selective college enrollment.
  • Nonselective colleges show similar results as in Table 4, implying results are driven by students at the lower tail.

Partial confirmation of verbal skills dominance

  • Paragraph comprehension is more responsive than arithmetic reasoning in college enrollment. (Table 8, [6])

This hints that, in the US, verbal skills may be relevant only up to college graduation but not beyond.
